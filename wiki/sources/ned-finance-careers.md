# Finance Careers Overview — Ned (365 Financial Analyst)

> **Author:** Ned, Founder and CEO of 365 Financial Analyst
> **Platform:** 365financialanalyst.com
> **Type:** Video transcript/career guide

## Summary

Ned provides a comprehensive overview of the major finance career paths and job roles available to finance graduates. He draws on his own experience working as a forex dealer, financial analyst, M&A advisor, and consultant. The video outlines six major fields: **corporate finance**, **investment banking**, **investment management**, **traditional banking**, **financial services**, and **consulting**. 

For each field, Ned describes the main job types, typical entry-level roles, career progression paths, and what makes each appealing. He emphasizes that each field demands specialized skills but offers numerous opportunities. A recurring theme: universities don't adequately prepare graduates for real-world finance work, which is why Ned founded 365 Financial Analyst to teach practical skills.

## Key Takeaways

- **Six major finance domains** exist, each with distinct roles, skills, and career trajectories.
- **Corporate finance** is the broadest field, encompassing roles in accounting, financial analysis, FP&A, and treasury. Entry-level financial analyst roles are generalist and excellent preparation.
- **Investment banking** focuses on capital raising, M&A advisory, and trading. M&A analysts are highly coveted but demanding roles. Many analysts move to PE/VC after initial years.
- **Investment management** includes venture capital and private equity. VC analysts source early-stage deals; PE analysts work on larger, mature-company acquisitions.
- **Traditional banking** centers on lending, credit assessment, and risk management. Relationship managers work directly with corporate clients.
- **Financial services and consulting** (Big Four: PwC, KPMG, EY, Deloitte) provide auditing, tax, transaction services, and advisory roles. These firms employ over 1 million people globally.
- **Career progression varies by field** but generally moves from analyst → manager → director → VP/partner.
- **Practical skills gap**: university education doesn't teach Excel, financial modeling, accounting, and real business strategy that the industry demands.
